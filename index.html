What makes a movie
financially successful?

We analyzed over a million films from the TMDB database to uncover the relationship between budget, ratings, and return on investment, and found that the story isn't as straightforward as you'd think.

1,405,001 raw entries → 8,181 high quality films analyzed
Motivation

Money can't buy love, or ratings.

We've all seen massive blockbusters that critics hated, and tiny indie films that audiences adored. But what does the data say? Is there a systematic relationship between how much a film costs, how well it's rated, and how much it earns back?

This investigation uses The Movie Database (TMDB), a publicly available dataset with roughly 1.4 million movie entries, updated daily. We focus on one central question: What drives return on investment in film, and when do ratings and profits tell different stories?

Key question: Can we identify patterns that separate financially successful films from acclaimed but unprofitable ones, and vice versa?
The Data

From 1.4 million rows to meaningful insight.

The raw TMDB dataset is vast, but also noisy. Many entries have missing budgets, implausible zero values, or ratings based on just a handful of votes. Before any analysis, we needed to carefully clean and filter the data.

We first selected eight key variables: budget, revenue, genres, title, vote average, vote count, runtime, and release date.


Then we applied a series of quality filters:

  • Vote count > 30, ensures ratings are statistically meaningful (removes the spike at perfect 10s with only 1 vote)
  • Runtime ≥ 60 minutes, excludes short films and non feature content
  • Budget & revenue > $0, treats zero values as missing data
  • Valid genre data, removes ~45% of entries with missing genre info

Budget

A mountain of modest budgets, and a few giants.

The budget distribution across our 8,181 films follows a striking pattern: the vast majority of films are made for under $50 million, with a long tail of big budget productions stretching into the hundreds of millions.

This exponential decay shape means the median film budget is far lower than the average, a few giant productions pull the mean upward while the typical movie operates on a much tighter budget.

Return on Investment

Big budgets don't guarantee big returns.

We calculated ROI as \[ ROI = \frac{revenue - budget}{budget} \] for each film. The results reveal a powerful pattern: the highest ROI films are overwhelmingly in the $10K-$20M budget range. Big budget films rarely achieve extreme ROI multiples.

Key insight: ROI is inherently sensitive to small budgets. A $50K film earning $500K achieves a 10x ROI, while a $200M blockbuster would need $2B to match that multiple. This mathematical reality concentrates high ROI outcomes in the low budget range.

Ratings

The curious case of the perfect 10.

Before filtering, the rating distribution showed a pretty good gaussian bell distribution with an some odd spikes at integer values (expected) and at the maximum value of 10.

Upon investigation, these perfect scores almost always came from films with only 1 or 2 votes, statistically meaningless. Given this, we implemented a vote count threshold to eliminate this artifact.

Genre

Drama dominates the "unprofitable but good" list.

When we break down the outlier groups by genre, a clear pattern emerges: Drama films are heavily overrepresented among the critically acclaimed but financially unsuccessful. Horror and Comedy appear more frequently in the "profitable but bad" group.

Why Drama? Dramas often tackle challenging subject matter that earns critical praise but struggles to attract mass audiences. Horror and comedy by contrast can attract peolpe easier, delivering strong returns on modest budgets even when reviews are mixed.

Timing

September: the month of acclaimed but unprofitable films.

Analyzing release months reveals a temporal pattern: "unprofitable but good" movies show a notable peak in September, often a month when studios release prestige, awards season contenders that may not prioritize box office returns.

Discussion

What we learned, and what we didn't.

This analysis succeeded in extracting a reliable subset from a very noisy dataset and identifying meaningful patterns between financial performance and audience perception. The iterative approach, identifying issues through exploration and responding with targeted filters, was key.

Strengths

  • Rigorous data cleaning driven by observed problems (vote count threshold, zero value handling)
  • Intuitive outlier definition based on disagreement between ROI and ratings
  • Coherent findings: high ROI concentration in low budgets, Drama's dominance among "unprofitable but good"
  • Combination of quantitative analysis and qualitative follow up on extreme outliers

Limitations

  • Treating zero values as missing likely biases toward commercial productions
  • ROI is inherently unstable for very small budgets (small absolute gains → large ratios)
  • Outlier thresholds (rating > 7, ROI > 1) are reasonable but somewhat arbitrary
  • Analysis remains descriptive, no formal statistical testing or predictive modeling was performed
Takeaway: The film industry rewards different things at different budget levels. At the low end, efficiency and audience appeal drive ROI. At the high end, critical acclaim and artistic ambition often come at a financial cost. The data doesn't give simple answers, but it does reveal consistent patterns worth understanding.
